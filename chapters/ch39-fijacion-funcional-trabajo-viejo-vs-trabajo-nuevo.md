# Fijación funcional + Trabajo viejo vs trabajo nuevo + Certeza ciega

Once you catalog something, it disappears as an option.

## Part 1: Functional Fixedness

### The 1945 Test

Karl Duncker, a German psychologist, ran an experiment in 1945.

He placed three objects in front of you: a candle, a box of thumbtacks, and a box of matches.

**The task:** Attach the candle to the wall so it doesn't drip wax on the table.

Most people try two ways. Nail the candle directly with the tacks. Or melt it with the matches and stick it.

Neither works well.

**The solution takes thirty seconds once you see it:** Empty the box of tacks, nail it to the wall, and use it as a holder.

People who hear it for the first time almost always react the same way: something like irritation.

**The answer was visible from the start.**

Duncker tested this the other way too. When he presented the box already empty—tacks removed—most people found the solution immediately.

Without assigned function, the box was visible as a holder. With assigned function, it became invisible.

### Why It Costs So Much

Because the box arrived as "container of tacks."

Your brain cataloged it. And once cataloged, it stops appearing as an option.

**Duncker called it functional fixedness:** the tendency to perceive objects only according to their usual function.

**Categories organize perception. And in organizing, they also limit it.**

What we see depends partly on what we decided each thing is.

### The Label as Boundary

Your support team exists to resolve incidents. That's what the category says, and the category closes possibilities.

Some companies discovered that the same team—the one spending the most hours talking to real customers—was their most valuable sales channel.

They didn't see it until they stopped calling it "support."

The same pattern repeats:
- The internal tool nobody thought of as a product
- The onboarding process that could be marketing content
- The pattern that could be a framework

**Functional fixedness doesn't operate only on physical objects.**

It operates on everything with a name in your business. The box of tacks has been there since day one.

**The problem is what's written on the label.**

## Part 2: Old Work vs. New Work

### The Distinction That Matters

**Old work** is what you already know how to do. Familiar, habitual, what you've executed for years. You can spend ten hours a day doing old work and feel physical exhaustion, but mental effort is lower because it's what you always do. Your body works, but your mind runs on autopilot.

**New work** is different. It's starting a project that didn't exist. Reinventing how your business works. Learning something you don't yet master. Sitting in front of a blank page and building something still without shape. Here the mental effort is enormous because there's no routine to lean on, no marked path to follow.

### Why Everyone Chooses Old Work

Most people fill their days with old work.

Not from laziness—the opposite. They work hard, many hours, with real dedication. But old work has a problem: **it feels productive.**

And the majority of their revenue comes from it.

So the incentive structure reinforces the trap.

**The market rewards what you've already mastered.** It doesn't reward what you're building. And as a result, expert entrepreneurs get trapped perfecting a version the market is leaving behind.

### The Transformation Door

Invest 30 minutes a day in new work.

Not 30 minutes stolen from sleep or life. 30 minutes taken from old work—the work that feels productive but generates linear returns.

That half hour might be the highest-return investment in your life.

Because new work, by definition, is where the transformation lives. And transformation compounds.

## Part 3: Blind Certainty

### The Invisible Prison

I've known entrepreneurs with enviable certainty. They knew exactly what worked, what didn't, where they were going.

And they went blind.

Not all at once. But gradually, they stopped seeing signals that contradicted their narrative.

Because certainty has a cost: **it kills your ability to perceive what's outside the frame you've already decided is reality.**

### Doubt as Capacity

**Doubt isn't indecision. It's the capacity to see more variables.**

The entrepreneurs who adapted fastest weren't those with the clearest vision. They were the ones who stayed uncertain long enough to see what was actually happening, not what they expected to happen.

Certainty is powerful. But it's most dangerous when it's wrong—because it blinds you to the signals that could correct you.

---

**Key Principle:**
Three forces limit your vision: (1) how you categorize things (labels), (2) what you've decided is your job (old work), (3) what you're certain about (blind spots).

Break one, and you see new options.
