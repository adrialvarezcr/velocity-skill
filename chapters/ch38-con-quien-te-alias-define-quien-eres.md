# Con quién te alías define quién eres

The difference between aligning to add audience and aligning to add dimensions.

## Core Principle

Watson and Crick discovered DNA in 1953 not because they were similar, but because they were radically different.

Watson was a biologist. Crick was a physicist.

Watson understood molecular biology but lacked the mathematical tools to model structure. Crick mastered crystallography and 3D modeling but didn't know the biological terrain.

Neither could arrive alone.

Together, they produced the most important discovery in modern biology. Not despite being different. Because they were different.

Each brought a dimension the other didn't have. And the intersection of both dimensions produced something that didn't exist in either one separately.

## The Default Collaboration: Addition vs. Intersection

Meanwhile, dozens of teams in universities worldwide worked on the same problem. Biologists with biologists. Chemists with chemists. Equals with equals. They produced incremental papers no one remembers.

The most transformative alliance in 20th-century science was between two people who shared no discipline, no training, no way of thinking.

**In the knowledge market, alliance logic works exactly the same way. But almost nobody applies it.**

## The Fallacy of Affinity

There's a dynamic that repeats between experts:

Someone with audience seeks another with audience. The logic seems simple: "You have 10,000 subscribers, I have 10,000. Together we reach 20,000."

Sounds good on a napkin. Almost never works in reality.

**The problem is deeper than you think:**

When two experts align because they have similar audience size, they usually also have similar positioning.

- They talk about similar topics
- They attract similar profiles
- They offer similar perspectives

The result isn't 10,000 + 10,000 = 20,000. The result is **overlap**. Same people receiving more of the same thing.

Neither audience discovers anything new.

**That's not an alliance. That's duplication.**

## What Watson and Crick Understood Without Saying It

The reason they discovered DNA wasn't that they "complemented" each other generically. It was more precise: **each made visible a dimension the other couldn't see.**

Watson looked at the biological problem and saw things Crick couldn't. Crick looked from physics and saw things Watson couldn't.

Together, the problem became three-dimensional—visible from angles neither had alone.

Alliances that multiply work the same way. **They don't add audiences. They add dimensions.**

- A business strategy expert + behavioral psychology expert = new perspective to both audiences
- A brand consultant + sales systems specialist = intersection that didn't exist
- That's what multiplies: the ability to make visible what was invisible, from an angle you alone can't offer

## Before You Ally With Anyone

Most experts choose allies by affinity. "We get along, we have similar audiences, let's merge."

**That's the wrong lens.**

Instead, ask:
- **What can they see that I can't?**
- **What dimension do they bring that makes my expertise legible in a new way?**
- **Will their audience discover something new from me, not just more of what they already know?**

The allies worth choosing are the ones who don't look like you, don't think like you, and don't serve the same market you serve.

They're the ones whose perspective, when intersected with yours, creates something neither of you could build alone.

---

**Key Insight:**
Positioning strategy is about who you build with, not just what you build. Your allies define your dimensions. Choose them not for comfort, but for complementary vision.
