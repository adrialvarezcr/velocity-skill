# Gastos que normalizas + Disciplina que no cuestionas

Not all costs are equal. Most of what's draining you is what you stopped questioning.

## Part 1: Linear Expenses vs. Multiplying Investments

### Not All Euros Are the Same

One of the biggest mistakes I see in entrepreneurs—and one I've made myself—is confusing linear expenses with investments that multiply.

In a profit-and-loss statement, both look identical. One line. One number. One monthly cost.

**But they produce completely different futures.**

### The Normalization Problem

What happens with linear expenses is you get used to them. You normalize them.

Office. Equipment. Tools. Subscriptions. Secondary locations. They become fixed lines in your P&L. Over time you attach to them. They become "the cost of doing business." And once normalized, they become invisible.

**You stop questioning whether they're producing anything.**

I've seen this over and over:

An entrepreneur afraid to invest €2,000 in strategic consulting or high-leverage courses, but carrying €20,000 monthly payroll where half the team occupies space without adding real value.

Secondary locations that aren't profitable. Subscriptions no one uses. Processes that exist because they've always existed.

The €2,000 feels like a risk. The €20,000 feels normal.

**But one has the potential to multiply. The other only drains.**

### What Experience Taught Me

I can trace most of my business skills and success to one specific moment: when I started investing in coaching in the United States.

A monthly investment. Nothing spectacular at the time.

But that investment became a mass of skills, judgment, and capabilities that compounded over years.

Every important decision I made afterward carried the fingerprint of that learning. It wasn't an expense—it was the entry price to every decision that followed.

Now I look at the agent platform we just created and are about to launch.

I look at its potential and consider the cost of building it. What's clear is the disproportion between investment and what it can generate.

The same with the investment we're making at Cadence House in book creation. For what you'd pay someone on your team to do low-leverage activities, that same money invested correctly can launch multiple product lines in a year.

### The Question That Changes Your P&L

**The question is not "Can I afford this?" The question is: Does this multiply or just sum?**

Linear expenses give you linear results. Multiplying investments give you disproportionate results.

Most businesses are overloaded with the first and starved of the second.

Look at your P&L. Not the total. The species of each line. How much is linear? How much multiplies? How much is there simply because it's always been there?

**That proportion tells you more about your future than the revenue number at the top.**

## Part 2: Discipline as Excuse

### When Discipline Becomes Blindness

I experienced this firsthand last week—a situation that repeats in many entrepreneurs.

I'd been executing with discipline for months. I produced, I published, I delivered. Religiously. Without skipping a week.

But suddenly results started stagnating. My first impulse was to continue, to maintain discipline.

Until I decided to pause and observe.

What changed my reading: **discipline had kept me productive, but inside a model the market was leaving behind.**

I was perfecting old work at increasing volume. The market was asking for something different.

### The Distinction

**Discipline is the ability to do what you decided yesterday, today.**

That's valuable. But only if what you decided yesterday is still the right move.

When the market shifts and you keep executing the same way, discipline isn't strength. **It's blindness disguised as consistency.**

### The Transformation Signal

The moment discipline becomes a problem isn't when you're slowing down. It's when you're speeding up doing the wrong thing.

When you look at the effort/results ratio and realize you're working more for less. That's not a sign to push harder.

**That's a signal to pause and ask: What have I stopped questioning?**

---

**Integration of Both Parts:**

1. **You normalize linear expenses.** They become invisible because they feel normal.
2. **You normalize linear work.** It becomes invisible because it feels productive.
3. **Discipline locks you into both.** You keep doing what you've always done because that's what you decided.

The breakthrough isn't in working harder or being more disciplined. It's in questioning what you've stopped questioning.

- Which expenses are actually multiplying?
- Which work is actually transforming?
- Which disciplines are still aligned with where the market is going?

**The answers usually aren't obvious. But the cost of not asking is compounding.**

---

**Key Principle:**

What's killing your growth usually isn't what's new. It's what's so normal you stopped seeing it.
